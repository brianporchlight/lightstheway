The Drive Home

The call ends. The paperwork closes. And then there's the part no one trains you for.

There's a stretch of road most first responders know without ever naming it. It's the distance between the last call of the shift and your own front door. On paper it's a commute. In practice, it's the most undefended part of the day — the moment the adrenaline finally drains out and leaves you sitting at a red light with everything you just saw still riding shotgun.

Nobody briefs you on the drive home. You're trained for the scene. You're drilled on the protocol. But the transition back into being a parent, a partner, a person — that you're expected to figure out on your own, somewhere between the parking lot and the porch.

Why the gap matters

The body doesn't have a clean off switch. The same systems that sharpened you for the worst moments of someone else's life don't power down the instant the call clears. They taper. And if you walk through your front door while they're still running, the people who love you meet the version of you that's built for emergencies, not for dinner.

You can't pour out at home what you never set down on the way there.

This is how good people end up feeling like strangers in their own kitchens. Not because they stopped caring, but because no one ever showed them that coming home is a skill, and like any skill, it can be practiced.

Making the drive a doorway

The most resilient responders I know treat that drive as a threshold, not dead time. Some roll the windows down for the first mile and let the shift physically blow off. Some have a single song that means it's over now. Some sit in the driveway for ninety seconds before they go in — three slow breaths, a hand on the wheel, a quiet acknowledgment of whatever the day asked of them.

None of this is magic, and none of it erases a hard call. What it does is mark a line. It tells the body that the readiness can stand down, and that the person walking inside gets to be soft again. The ritual matters less than the fact that you have one.

If you don't have a line yet, you can build one tonight. Pick the smallest version — one breath in the driveway — and let it be enough to start. The drive home was always going to happen. The only question is whether you let it carry you back to yourself.

A note before you go. If the weight of the work is following you home more often than not — or if reading this landed a little too close — that's worth taking seriously, and you don't have to sort it out alone.
