Why We Don't Reach Out Sooner

On why we wait, and what it costs to carry it alone.

I have thought a lot about why people don't reach out sooner. Not just firefighters. Not just first responders. People in general. But in the fire service, I think there is something specific about the culture, the schedule, the identity, and the way we learn to move through hard things.

We are trained to keep going. The next call. The next shift. The next overtime opportunity. The next drill. The next skill. The next thing we need to handle. We spend so much of our lives responding, fixing, preparing, improving, providing, and pushing through that sitting still long enough to take a good look at ourselves can feel almost impossible.

And maybe that is part of why we wait. Because if we slow down, if we reach out, if we let someone start digging around in there with us, we might find something we are not ready to find yet.

We tell ourselves we are okay. We tell ourselves it is just a rough shift. We are tired. We did not sleep. We worked too much. We are busy. We have responsibilities. We have people counting on us. We don't have time to take an hour out of the day to sit with someone and talk about what is really going on. Though we will do that at the firehouse with each other, but that is different.

And honestly, sometimes we don't see the benefit yet. When your whole life is built around getting things done, helping others, fixing problems, and moving forward, taking time to focus on yourself can feel strange. It can feel selfish. It can feel unnecessary. It can feel like one more thing on a list that is already too full.

We are good at pushing past the moment we are actually in. We keep looking toward the next thing. The next call. The next shift. The next class. The next promotion. The next project. The next version of who we think we are supposed to become. We get so used to moving forward that we forget to ask a simple question.

How am I actually doing inside?

That question is hard, and the answer might be rough to hear.

There is a reason someone becomes a firefighter, a medic, a police officer, a dispatcher, a counselor, a nurse, a helper. There is something in us that is drawn toward the moment when things are not okay. We want to be useful there. We want to be steady there. We want to know what to do when other people are scared, hurt, overwhelmed, or lost. There is a reason we find these professions, and maybe that is a topic for another piece, but it matters here, because the same thing that pulls us toward helping others can also make it harder to see when we are the ones who need help.

That is not a bad thing. It is actually one of the most honorable parts of the job. But that same wiring can make it hard to turn around and see ourselves clearly.

We know how to help someone else. We know how to recognize when a brother or sister seems off. We can see the irritability. The isolation. The short fuse. The drinking too much. The joking that does not feel like joking anymore. The person who is showing up, but not really there. We can say, something is going on with them. We can step in. We can make the call. We can get them connected.

That part is easier for us, because it still lets us be the helper. It is much harder when the person who needs help is us. That is the part we miss.

It is not always stubbornness. It is not always pride. It is not that we don't know better. A lot of times, we just don't see it. Not at first. We think we have more time. We think we can keep going. We think we can carry it a little longer. We think we can get through this season, this run of bad sleep, this stretch of overtime, this conflict at home, this heaviness we cannot quite name. And because we can still function, we believe we are okay.

The weight we carry

A firefighter can be carrying a lot and still look completely capable.

A firefighter can answer calls, lead their crew, make dinner at the station, train hard, joke on the truck, pick up overtime, go home, take care of their family, and still be quietly losing ground inside. From the outside, everything looks fine. From the inside, they may be tired in a way sleep does not fix. Angry in a way they don't understand. Numb in places they used to feel. Disconnected from the people they love and not even sure when it started.

The weight can become familiar. That is the part I think we do not talk about enough. Sometimes we carry things for so long that the weight starts to feel like part of who we are. Not because we want to suffer. Not because we are trying to be a martyr. Not because we think pain makes us better than anyone else. But because carrying hard things has always been part of how we understand ourselves.

We can carry it. We can take it. We can handle it. We can keep it together.

There is something that almost feels honorable about being able to hold more than most people see. We do not always say that out loud, but it is there. We respect the person who can take the hard call, work the long shift, stay calm in the worst moments, keep their family moving, and still show up again tomorrow. We admire that. We train for that. We praise that.

And again, some of that matters. The job requires strength. It requires endurance. It requires the ability to stay steady when things are not steady around you. But there is a difference between carrying something with purpose and carrying something because we don't know how to put it down.

The line we miss

There is a difference between being strong and being buried.

I think a lot of us wait because we do not realize how much life is being taken up by what we are carrying. We don't stop long enough to imagine what life could feel like without the constant weight. We are so used to managing it, adjusting to it, working around it, and explaining it away that we forget there might be another way to live.

Not perfect. Not pain free. Not untouched by the job. Just lighter. More present. More connected. More ourselves.

When others notice

Usually, before we can see it clearly, someone else sees the change first. A lot of times it starts at home.

Our kids

They feel it before they have the words for it. You're different. You do not laugh anymore. You never have time. You are always tired. Those words hit hard, because they are just telling us what it feels like to love us when we are not fully there.

Our partner

They may say, you are here, but you are not really here. They notice the distance, the irritability, the short fuse, the way we come home but don't really come back to them. The focus has drifted.

The crew

The edginess. The irritability. The short fuse. The way we are quicker to snap or slower to laugh. Maybe they notice we are around, but we are not really ourselves anymore.

And when enough people start reflecting that back to us, it becomes harder to keep dismissing it. And when that lands, it lands hard. At first, we argue with it. No, I am not different. I am just tired. I had a rough shift. I did not sleep well. I worked a double. I am just getting through this week.

And maybe some of that is true. But sometimes it is also true that the change has been happening slowly. So slowly we did not notice it. We became more distant, more impatient, more numb, more reactive, more disconnected. Not all at once. Not in a way that was easy to name. Just little by little, until one day the people who love us start reflecting back a version of ourselves we never meant to become.

That is a painful moment. Because none of us set out to become grumpy, shut down, irritable, or hard to reach. We do not want to bring the worst parts of the job home. We don't want the people we love to feel like they are getting what is left of us. We don't want to lose ourselves while still looking functional to everyone else.

But it happens. And when it finally lands, when we finally hear it, when we finally stop defending long enough to feel the truth in it, that is usually when something starts to open, or break, within.

When help matters

That is the moment when help matters most. Not the kind of help that feels cold or complicated. Not the kind that makes someone feel like a case, a liability, or a problem to manage. Not the kind that requires a person to already know exactly what is wrong before they are allowed to ask for support.

Real help has to feel different. It has to feel safe enough for someone to say, I do not know what is happening to me, but I know I am not okay. It has to feel human enough that they do not feel judged for waiting so long. It has to feel confidential, steady, and close enough that reaching out does not feel like one more hard thing they have to figure out on their own.

Because a lot of us do wait. We wait because we are busy surviving, performing, helping, fixing, providing, and convincing ourselves we are okay. We wait because we are wired to see the need in everyone else before we see it in ourselves. We wait because the weight has become familiar, and familiar can start to feel normal. We wait because looking inward is hard. We wait because part of us knows that if we stop moving, we may finally have to feel what has been catching up to us.

And when that day comes, when the words finally come, when the weight finally gets heavy enough to name, I hope there is somewhere to turn. I hope there is a person, a place, or a path that feels steady.

I hope there is a light left on.

Brian French
Licensed Professional Counselor · Phoenix Firefighter
If this is sitting close to home. You do not have to have the words yet, and you do not have to wait until the weight gets heavier. Reaching out sooner is not weakness. It is the steadiest thing you can do. There is a light left on.
